§ 01 — The Voice of a Drum

Strike, Listen,
Decompose

Strike the drum below — anywhere. The membrane's displacement immediately resolves into a superposition of normal modes:

u(x,y,t) = Σₙ aₙ · φₙ(x,y) · cos(√λₙ · ct)

The coefficients aₙ = ⟨u₀, φₙ⟩ are the inner products of your initial displacement with each eigenfunction — a spatial Fourier transform. The bar chart shows these amplitudes decaying as the drum rings down.

Strike near the center to excite only radially symmetric modes (m=0). Strike off-center to wake asymmetric modes (m≥1).

§ 02 — Fourier as Rotating Vectors

The Phasor
Constellation

Each eigenfrequency ωₙ = √λₙ · c/R of the drum corresponds to a phasor — a rotating vector. The tip of each phasor traces a circle; adding them produces the waveform heard at the drum's center.

This is the Fourier representation in the frequency domain. Toggle modes to add or remove their circular rotation. The trace below shows the resulting superposition over time.

The spectrum of a drum is exactly the set of frequencies whose phasors appear in this diagram. Two drums with the same spectrum produce the same superposition — they sound identical.
§ 03 — Eigenmodes of the Circular Drum

The Shapes of
Pure Sound

For a circular drum of radius R clamped at the boundary, the eigenfunctions are exact Bessel modes:

φₘₙ(r,θ) = Jₘ(jₘₙ · r/R) · cos(mθ) λₘₙ = (jₘₙ/R)² [eigenvalue = squared frequency]

§ 05 — The Spectrum Encodes Area

Weyl's Law:
You Can Hear the Area

Hermann Weyl proved in 1911 that the density of eigenvalues encodes the area. Let N(λ) count eigenvalues below λ. Then:

N(λ) ∼ Area(Ω) · λ / (4π) as λ → ∞

For the unit disk (R=1, Area = π), this gives N(λ) ∼ λ/4. The staircase is drawn live — watch each eigenvalue click into place. Higher-order Weyl terms recover perimeter, corners, and topology.

"From the asymptotic growth of a sequence of numbers, Weyl extracted a continuous geometric quantity. The finite encodes the infinite." — Spectral geometry tradition

§ 06 — Two Drums, One Voice

You Cannot Always
Hear the Shape

In 1992, Gordon, Webb and Wolpert exhibited two planar polygons — each assembled from seven right triangles — that share every eigenvalue of −Δ with Dirichlet boundary conditions, yet are not congruent. They vibrate below in sync, sharing each eigenmode's frequency.

The construction uses Sunada's transplantation method: an explicit linear isomorphism between eigenspaces on both domains, built from a group-covering argument. Zelditch later showed (2000) that among analytic domains with bilateral symmetry, the spectrum does determine the shape — making the generic case an active open problem.
